    <h1>Cyber</h1>
    <p class="page-subtitle">The prefix that launders nonsense into a budget line item</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Word Itself</h2>
    <p>At some point, someone discovered that prefixing anything with "cyber" would add a six-figure budget line and prevent anyone from asking follow-up questions. CyberSecurity. CyberDefense. CyberHygiene. CyberResilience. CyberPosture. These are not things. They are the word "IT" wearing a trench coat.</p>

    <p>The original Cybersecurity Director at your company learned about "the cyber" from a Senate hearing transcript and a conference keynote in 2014. They have not opened a terminal. They have opened a lot of PowerPoint decks with dark backgrounds and red lock icons.</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Vendor Playbook</h2>
    <p>Every vendor with a product made between 1997 and today has added "cyber" to their marketing materials. Firewall vendor? Now it's a Cyber Perimeter Defense Platform. Log aggregator? Cyber Threat Intelligence Hub. A bash script that checks if a port is open? Cyber Attack Surface Management Solution.</p>

    <p>The sales pitch is always the same: nation-state actors are targeting companies exactly like yours, right now, and only by purchasing a three-year enterprise license can you achieve Cyber Resilience. The demo will have a spinning globe with red lines on it. The lines mean threats. There are always a lot of lines.</p>

    <div class="quote-box">
        <blockquote>
            <p>"We need to invest in our cyber."</p>
            <cite>— A VP, to a room full of engineers who all made the same face at the same time</cite>
        </blockquote>
    </div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>What "Cyber" Usually Means in Practice</h2>
    <p>Strip the word out and translate: "CyberSecurity incident" means someone clicked a phishing link. "CyberRisk assessment" means a consultant ran Nessus and handed you a spreadsheet. "CyberAwareness training" means a mandatory 45-minute video that employees click through in 4 minutes and immediately forget. "Cyber Insurance" means your CFO found out what ransomware is.</p>

    <p>None of this is wrong, exactly. The phishing link was real. The Nessus report has real findings. The ransomware risk is real. But somewhere between the actual problem and the solution, someone added the word "cyber" and charged three times as much for the same thing.</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The CISO's Dilemma</h2>
    <p>The people who actually know what they are doing — the ones who can read a packet capture, explain why your JWT implementation is broken, or enumerate exactly what an attacker can do with that exposed metadata endpoint — those people do not say "cyber." They say "authentication," "authorization," "CVE-2024-something," "your S3 bucket is public."</p>

    <p>The CISO who learned the vocabulary from keynotes and vendor briefings says "cyber" fourteen times in the all-hands and then asks the senior engineer to "translate that into business terms." The senior engineer's translation is: "the door is unlocked." The CISO says they need a Cyber Door Risk Management Framework. The engineer updates their resume.</p>

    <div class="fun-facts">
        <h3>Cyber Glossary</h3>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>Cyber hygiene</strong> — patching, not using "password123", concepts from 2003</li>
            <li><strong>Cyber posture</strong> — how bad things are, expressed without specifics</li>
            <li><strong>Cyber resilience</strong> — surviving the breach you were supposed to prevent</li>
            <li><strong>Cyber threat actor</strong> — someone broke in; "actor" makes it sound like a film</li>
            <li><strong>Cyber awareness</strong> — the annual video nobody watches</li>
            <li><strong>Cyber strategy</strong> — a deck with no implementation plan</li>
        </ul>
    </div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>In Summary</h2>
    <p>CYBER.</p>

    <p>There. That's the whole thing. Add it to a word. Present it to leadership. Receive funding. The engineers will handle the actual security work later, between incidents, in the ten minutes per sprint allocated for "tech debt."</p>
</div>
