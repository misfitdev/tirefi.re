<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Title</h2>
    <p>At some point in the early 2010s, "DevOps" stopped being a description of a practice and became a job title. Then it became a senior job title. Then it became a director title. The comma-first construction — ", Director of DevOps" — captures the grammatical awkwardness of appending a methodology to an org chart, like hiring a Vice President of Agile or a Chief of Scrum.</p>

    <p>Pete Cheslock, for what it's worth, was actually doing the work. He was writing Chef cookbooks and thinking seriously about how software gets built and deployed, at a time when most organizations were still treating operations as a ticket queue. The tiara is the joke, but the joke only lands because he earned it — he was deep enough in the thing to be photographed wearing a crown at a conference, which is a specific kind of credibility that no LinkedIn endorsement can replicate.</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Inflation</h2>
    <p>The problem with "Director of DevOps" as a title is that DevOps was explicitly a critique of organizational structure — the argument was that separating development and operations created perverse incentives and slow feedback loops. Making it a separate department with a director reporting to a VP is the corporate immune system at work. The organization absorbed the concept and neutralized it by giving it a budget and a reporting line.</p>

    <p>This is not unique to DevOps. It happened to Agile. It is happening to platform engineering. The pattern is: practitioners develop a set of ideas about how work should be organized, those ideas spread, organizations adopt the vocabulary without the underlying changes, and eventually the practitioners need a new word for what they were originally describing. The tiaras accumulate.</p>
</div>
