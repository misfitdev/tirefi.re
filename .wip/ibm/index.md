    <h1>Big Blue Forever</h1>
    <p class="page-subtitle">pivoting since 1993, still somehow winning government contracts</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Company That Will Outlast the Sun</h2>
    <p>IBM was founded in 1911. The sun will burn out in approximately five billion years. IBM will still be billing someone for mainframe support when that happens. The contract will have auto-renewed seventeen times and no one will know whose budget it comes from.</p>

    <p>In procurement circles there's a saying: "No one ever got fired for buying IBM." This was true in 1985. It remains technically true today, not because IBM is the right choice, but because everyone who would fire you also bought IBM, and firing you would invite scrutiny of their own decisions.</p>

    <div class="quote-box">
        <blockquote>
            <p>"We're going to be strategic partners with IBM on this initiative."</p>
            <cite>— Your VP, six weeks before a reorg that won't affect the IBM contract</cite>
        </blockquote>
    </div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Pivot Tally</h2>
    <p>IBM has reinvented itself so many times that "reinvention" has lost meaning as a word in their press releases. Here is an incomplete accounting:</p>

    <ul>
        <li><strong>1911:</strong> Tabulating machines. Serious business. Government contracts.</li>
        <li><strong>1950s:</strong> Mainframes. The entire computing industry, basically.</li>
        <li><strong>1981:</strong> The PC. IBM invents it, immediately loses control of it to Microsoft and Intel.</li>
        <li><strong>1993:</strong> Lou Gerstner arrives. "IBM is a services company now." First pivot.</li>
        <li><strong>2002:</strong> PwC Consulting acquisition. "IBM is definitely a services company."</li>
        <li><strong>2005:</strong> Sells the PC division to Lenovo. That chapter is closed.</li>
        <li><strong>2014:</strong> Watson. "IBM is an AI company." This was the first time IBM said this.</li>
        <li><strong>2019:</strong> Red Hat acquisition for $34 billion. "IBM is a hybrid cloud company."</li>
        <li><strong>2021:</strong> Spins off managed infrastructure into Kyndryl. "IBM is a... different kind of company."</li>
        <li><strong>2023:</strong> "IBM is an AI company." This was not the first time IBM said this.</li>
    </ul>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Mainframe, Which Is Not Going Away</h2>
    <p>The IBM Z mainframe is a genuine engineering achievement. It processes a significant fraction of the world's financial transactions. It has been "about to be replaced" for forty consecutive years. It will not be replaced. The people who understand it are retiring, and they are not being replaced either, which is a different kind of problem that IBM is not currently pivoting to solve.</p>

    <p>zLinux runs Linux workloads on mainframe hardware. It was genuinely ahead of its time. The sales pitch has been "zLinux is going to be big this year" for roughly two decades. It is not big. It exists, which is more than can be said for most things IBM has announced in the interim.</p>

    <div class="quote-box">
        <blockquote>
            <p>"The mainframe isn't legacy infrastructure. It's proven, mission-critical infrastructure that modern enterprises rely on for their most demanding workloads."</p>
            <cite>— IBM, correctly, while also trying to sell you Watson</cite>
        </blockquote>
    </div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>Watson: A Case Study in Managing Expectations</h2>
    <p>Watson beat Ken Jennings at Jeopardy in 2011. This was impressive. IBM then announced Watson would cure cancer, revolutionize healthcare, transform financial services, and reinvent marketing. Watson did none of these things at the scale promised. Watson Health was sold in 2022. Watson is now watsonx, which is IBM's AI platform for the current pivot cycle.</p>

    <p>None of this is a failure, exactly. It is IBM working as designed: generate enormous enthusiasm around a technology, sell that enthusiasm to enterprise procurement, collect fees for years, quietly restructure when the gap between promise and delivery becomes awkward, and pivot to the next thing. The mainframe people used to call this "the IBM way." They meant it as a complaint. IBM heard it as a compliment.</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Honest Assessment</h2>
    <p>IBM's actual strengths are substantial and underreported: they hold more US patents than almost any other company, they run infrastructure that genuinely cannot go down, and their consulting arm has institutional knowledge that would take decades to rebuild. None of this is exciting. None of this goes in a TechCrunch headline.</p>

    <p>The enterprise knows this. That's why the contracts keep renewing. It is not naive vendor loyalty. It is the sober calculation that IBM will be there in ten years, the alternative might not be, and the switching costs are terrifying regardless. IBM has monetized this calculation for over a century. They're very good at it.</p>

    <p>In procurement they say no one ever got fired for buying IBM. What they don't say is that plenty of people have gotten fired for the IBM implementation going sideways. The contract survives. The implementation team does not.</p>
</div>
