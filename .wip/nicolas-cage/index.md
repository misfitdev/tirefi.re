    <h1>Nicolas Cage is Not a Tire Fire</h1>
    <p class="page-subtitle">he just plays one on TV</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>A National Treasure</h2>
    <p>Nicolas Cage has appeared in over 100 films. Some of them are among the best films ever made. Some of them are unwatchable. He has been in both categories, sometimes in the same year, occasionally in the same film. He commits completely to every role regardless of the script's quality, which is either admirable or insane, and the answer is both.</p>

    <p>This is exactly what working in ops is like.</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Ops Parallels, Documented</h2>
    <p><strong>Wildly unpredictable.</strong> Nicolas Cage turned down the role of Neo in The Matrix and took the role of Big Daddy in Kick-Ass at the same time. Production infrastructure makes decisions with the same apparent logic: the component you were confident about fails; the ancient system you were planning to replace just keeps running.</p>

    <p><strong>Occasionally genius.</strong> Leaving Las Vegas. Adaptation. Raising Arizona. Mandy. These are films that demonstrate genuine artistic capability deployed at full intensity. Your production infrastructure also has moments of genuine elegance — the cron job someone wrote in 2008 that handles exactly the edge case the new system was built to handle, doing it perfectly, in eleven lines of bash.</p>

    <p><strong>Always committed.</strong> Nicolas Cage does not coast. He does not show up and give 60%. Whatever he's doing, he is doing it completely, including the films where "completely" means completely unhinged. The on-call engineer at 3am is also always committed. They are not choosing to half-fix the incident. They are going as hard as they can at whatever is in front of them, with whatever context they have, on no sleep.</p>

    <p><strong>Refuses to be contained.</strong> The studios have tried. The critics have tried. Nicolas Cage continues to make choices that defy categorization and expectation. Similarly, your infrastructure has refused to be contained by every abstraction layer, every migration plan, every "this will simplify things" initiative that has been applied to it.</p>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>Face/Off as Infrastructure Migration Metaphor</h2>
    <p>Face/Off is a 1997 film in which Nicolas Cage and John Travolta exchange faces through an elaborate surgical procedure and proceed to inhabit each other's lives while trying to destroy each other. It is completely sincere. It is completely committed. It is objectively absurd and also somehow works.</p>

    <p>This is every major infrastructure migration. You take the workloads from System A and put them on System B through an elaborate migration procedure. The teams switch roles. The old system starts behaving like the new one was supposed to. The new system starts having the problems the old system had. By the end, no one is entirely sure which system they're operating, the original problems have been replaced by different problems of equivalent severity, and John Travolta is flying a plane.</p>

    <div class="quote-box">
        <blockquote>
            <p>"I'd like to take his face... off."</p>
            <cite>— Nicolas Cage, Face/Off. Also: any senior engineer looking at the current infrastructure diagram.</cite>
        </blockquote>
    </div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>What We Can Learn</h2>
    <p>Nicolas Cage has won an Academy Award. He has also been in Wicker Man (2006), which contains one of the most famous scenes in cinema history for reasons unrelated to quality. He did not let Wicker Man define him. He kept making films. Some of them were Mandy.</p>

    <p>The incidents will not define you either. The 3am pages, the failed migrations, the postmortems, the thing that broke in a way that had never been imagined and took eight hours to diagnose — these are the Wicker Man films. Keep going. Mandy is out there.</p>

    <p>This man is a national treasure. So is everyone who keeps production running.</p>
</div>
