</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Celebration</h2>
    <p>System Administrator Appreciation Day was established in 2000 to recognize the people who keep the lights on. It is observed annually on the last Friday of July. It is observed primarily by sysadmins forwarding the Wikipedia article to each other.</p>

    <p>This year, the CEO sent a Slack message. It said "shoutout to the IT team!" The IT team is not the ops team. The ops team runs the infrastructure that the entire company depends on to function. The IT team handles laptop provisioning and VPN access. They are different teams. The CEO cannot name anyone on either team.</p>

    <div class="quote-box">
        <blockquote>
            <p>"Happy SysAdmin Day to all those who labor in darkness so the rest of us can work in the light. You know who you are (because we don't)."</p>
            <cite>— every all-hands message, more or less</cite>
        </blockquote>
    </div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>What Acknowledgment Actually Looks Like</h2>
    <p>In a good year: someone from the dev team says "hey thanks" in the ops channel and means it. This is the ceiling. This is as good as it gets. Treasure it.</p>

    <p>In a normal year: the Slack message from the CEO. Maybe a gift card for a coffee chain in a city where nobody on the ops team lives. A note in the company newsletter that refers to the ops team as "the unsung heroes keeping everything running," which is accurate but would carry more weight if it were followed by headcount or budget.</p>

    <div class="fun-facts">
        <h3>Forms of Appreciation Ranked by Actual Value</h3>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>Headcount:</strong> You are so appreciated we hired another person so you can sleep sometimes. Extremely rare.</li>
            <li><strong>Budget:</strong> You are so appreciated we approved the tooling request that's been in the queue since Q1. Rare.</li>
            <li><strong>Genuine thanks from a peer who understands the work:</strong> More valuable than it sounds. Costs nothing. Happens infrequently enough to be meaningful.</li>
            <li><strong>Company newsletter mention:</strong> Noted. Filed. Forgotten by Q3 planning.</li>
            <li><strong>Slack message from a VP who had to be reminded it was SysAdmin Day:</strong> You can see the reminder in the metadata if you look at the timestamp versus when they usually post.</li>
            <li><strong>Gift card to a coffee chain:</strong> Worth exactly its face value, minus the friction of remembering to use it.</li>
            <li><strong>Being called "IT" when you are not IT:</strong> The dark timeline.</li>
        </ul>
    </div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
    <h2>The Cost Center Problem</h2>
    <p>Operations is a cost center. This is the accounting designation. It means: money flows in, nothing billable flows out. You are an expense on a spreadsheet. This is technically accurate and completely wrong as a description of what you do.</p>

    <p>The cost center framing means ops is always "how do we reduce this" instead of "how do we invest in this." It means the team is sized for normal conditions and not for the incidents that will happen. It means the budget conversation every year starts from "justify your existence" instead of "what do you need."</p>

    <p>Your on-call rotation is running on three people. There used to be five. The other two left. The headcount request has been "under review" for two quarters. Happy SysAdmin Day.</p>

    <div class="quote-box">
        <blockquote>
            <p>"I am Jack's cost center. I am Jack's completely taken-for-granted uptime. I am Jack's ignored PagerDuty alert at 3am."</p>
            <cite>— a sysadmin, July 29th, any year</cite>
        </blockquote>
    </div>
</div>
